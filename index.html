Our Goal — What This Observatory Does

This is a live research tool for detecting and studying the two-stream instability (TSI) in real NASA spacecraft data. We combine rigorous theoretical analysis with direct MMS observations to answer a fundamental question: when two electron beams collide in the magnetosphere, can we see the instability grow in real time?

The page has two complementary parts. The Theory Laboratory (sections 01–07 below) contains the exact dispersion equations from our paper — non-relativistic and relativistic, single and multi-species. The MMS Observatory (sections 08 onward) downloads real magnetic and plasma data from NASA's MMS-1 spacecraft every 6 hours and tests whether the theoretical conditions for instability are actually met.

We specifically search for four observational signatures of TSI: (1) electron holes — bipolar E∥ pulses from nonlinear saturation; (2) a positive df/dv slope in the electron distribution (bump-on-tail); (3) the TSI→Weibel causal chain — does beam activity precede temperature anisotropy?; (4) Langmuir wave enhancement near the plasma frequency. We also log unusual plasma events and validate measured growth rates against predictions from Equations 18–21.

Physics Concepts

⚡

The Setup

Two beams of electrons travel in opposite directions at ±v₀. Each carries half the total density. A tiny density perturbation δn·cos(kx) seeds the instability.

n±(x) = n₀/2 · [1 + δn·cos(kx)]
📈

Why It Grows

When the resonance condition ω/k ≈ v₀ is met, particles and electric waves exchange energy coherently. Small perturbations amplify exponentially at rate γ.

E(t) ∝ e^(γt) → Im(ω) = γ > 0
🌀

Nonlinear Saturation

Growth stops when particles become trapped in wave potential wells — "cat's eye" vortices in x–pₓ phase space. Beam energy converts to wave energy, then heat.

t_sat ~ 3/γ · ln(1/δn)

FAQ + Physical Intuition

Why does the instability happen at all? ▼
Two beams create a free energy reservoir. When wave phase velocity ω/k ≈ v₀, particles and waves exchange energy resonantly. Slow particles get accelerated; faster ones decelerate — net energy flows from beams to wave amplitude, driving exponential growth.
Why is there a k_max (peak growth wavenumber)? ▼
Low k: resonance far from plasma frequency → weak coupling. High k: 2v₀²k² term dominates bracket → bracket becomes positive → real ω → stable oscillation, no growth. Peak growth at optimal k_max where resonance coupling is strongest before stabilization. This peak shifts to lower k as v₀ increases.
How does relativity suppress growth? ▼
The Lorentz factor γ₀³ appears in the denominator of ωₚ² terms. At high v₀, γ₀→large, so effective plasma frequency ωₚ/γ₀^(3/2) → small. At v₀→c: γ₀→∞, growth rate→0. Relativistic inertia stabilizes the beam.
What are the "cat's eyes" in phase space? ▼
When wave amplitude is large, particles trapped in potential wells execute closed orbits in (x, pₓ) — these look like elliptical cat's eyes. Trapping width Δpₓ ~ √(eE/k). When trapping occurs, linear theory fails and growth saturates.
Multi-species vs single-species — what changes? ▼
Single-species: ions are frozen (infinite mass). Multi-species: mobile ions add an extra ωₚ² term, effectively doubling the ωₚ coefficient in the dispersion. This slightly increases growth rates and shifts k cutoffs. Ion dynamics become important at late times — beam filamentation and ion-acoustic coupling.
What does the "bracket" condition mean? ▼
γ = √{½·bracket}. bracket < 0 → √(negative) → imaginary ω → Im(ω)=γ>0 → exponential growth. bracket > 0 → real ω → stable oscillation. The k_cutoff is exactly where bracket = 0.
How do SMILEI parameters map to this theory? ▼
grid_length = 2π/k (exactly one wavelength, xnumber=1 ✓)
cell_length = Δx ≪ λ_Debye (typically 0.01–0.05)
timestep = 0.0095 (CFL: Δt < Δx/c)
solve_poisson = True: critical — without this Ex=0 at t=0, instability never seeds
Simulate to T ~ 5/γ to capture linear + nonlinear phases
Two-stream vs Weibel instability? ▼
Two-stream drives electrostatic (longitudinal) waves — Ex grows, from charge bunching. Weibel instability is electromagnetic (transverse) — grows from momentum anisotropy, generating magnetic filaments. Weibel dominates in oblique geometries or high transverse temperature. Both can coexist and thermalize beams.

Electron Holes — TSI Saturation Evidence

EDP bipolar E∥ pulses · nonlinear phase products
📖 Why electron holes prove TSI happened
When the two-stream instability grows, Langmuir waves amplify exponentially. Once their electric potential φ ~ T_e/e, they trap electrons in "potential wells" that travel along the magnetic field at the beam speed. These are electron phase-space holes — localised regions of depleted electron density, positive potential, and bipolar (positive-then-negative) parallel electric field.

MMS EDP resolves them because they pass the spacecraft in ~1–50 ms. Detection criterion: a bipolar E∥ event where |E∥| > 3×median(|E∥|), duration 0.1–500 ms, and the waveform crosses zero twice within the event. Each detected hole is a fossil of a Langmuir wave that saturated — direct proof that TSI reached its nonlinear phase.

References: Bernstein et al. (1957) PRL; Fox et al. (2012) PRL; Graham et al. (2016) GRL; Steinvall et al. (2021) GRL — MMS observations
Detection algorithm — bipolar E∥ pulse identification
Threshold: |E∥| > 3 × median(|E∥|) and |E∥| > 0.5 mV/m (avoids noise floor)
Duration: 0.1 ms < Δt < 500 ms (EDP at 32 sps → resolves > 31 ms)
Shape: waveform crosses zero before and after peak (bipolar signature)
Speed: v_hole ≈ v_beam (measured ≈ 100–1000 km/s from multi-spacecraft timing)
Debye size: L_hole / λ_D typically 3–20 (sub-Debye to several Debye lengths)

Electron Beam & VDF Slope Analysis

FPI-DES moments · bump-on-tail test · 2025 debate
📖 The bump-on-tail condition and the 2025 debate
Classical TSI theory (our Eq.18–21) requires a positive slope in the electron velocity distribution function at the wave phase velocity: ∂f/∂v > 0 at v = v_phase = ω_pe/k. This is the "bump-on-tail" configuration — a second population of faster electrons creates a local maximum in f(v).

However, a July 2025 paper (Jebaraj et al. 2025, A&A) argues that many in-situ observations are better explained by a truncated distribution (the beam is cut off by time-of-flight dispersion, not by a thermal bump). In this case, df/dv ≤ 0 everywhere but the sharp cutoff still drives Langmuir waves via a different mechanism.

We estimate the bump-on-tail proxy as r = v_beam/v_thermal. If r >> 1, a classical bump is expected. If r ≈ 1, only truncation. From MMS FPI-DES: v_thermal = √(2 T_par / m_e) computed from measured T_par.

References: Buneman (1958) PRL; Jebaraj et al. (2025) A&A — bump-on-tail vs truncation debate
Key formulae
Thermal speed: v_th = √(2 k_B T_par / m_e) = √(2 T_par[eV] / 511000) × c [km/s]
Beam-thermal ratio: r = V_e / v_th → bump-on-tail if r ≫ 1
Resonance: k_res = ω_pe / V_e (beam resonance condition k·v₀ = ω_pe)
Plasma frequency: f_pe [Hz] = 8980 × √(n_e [cm⁻³])

TSI → Weibel Energy Chain

Cross-correlation analysis · T⊥/T∥ · causal test
📖 The TSI→Weibel causal chain (Innocenti et al. 2017)
PIC simulations predict a direct energy cascade: electron beam → two-stream instability → Langmuir waves grow → waves scatter beam electrons into the perpendicular direction → builds T_perp > T_par → drives Weibel-like electromagnetic waves that generate magnetic fluctuations from a seed field of essentially zero.

Observational signature: a spike in V_e (beam active → TSI growing) should be followed — with a short lag — by a rise in T_perp/T_par (perpendicular heating → Weibel drive building up). We test this with cross-correlation analysis: C(τ) = ⟨V_e(t) × [T⊥/T∥](t+τ)⟩. A positive peak at τ > 0 confirms the causal ordering.

This chain has not been directly confirmed observationally. Confirming it with MMS data would be a publishable result.

References: Innocenti et al. (2017) ApJL; Weibel (1959) PRL; MMS FPI-DES measurements
Cross-correlation test and Weibel threshold
Cross-correlation: C(τ) = ⟨V_e(t) × [T⊥/T∥](t+τ)⟩ / (σ_Ve × σ_T)
Chain detected if: C(τ) > 0.2 AND peak_lag τ > 0 AND beam_flag = True AND A > 0.05
Weibel parameter: A = T_perp/T_par − 1 (must be > 0 for instability)
Weibel growth rate: γ_W / ω_p = √(A/(1+A)) × v_th_perp / c
Firehose threshold: A < −1/β_∥ (opposite instability if T_par ≫ T_perp)

Langmuir Wave Proxy

EDP electric field PSD · linear phase TSI product
📖 Langmuir waves — the first sign that TSI is active
In the linear phase of TSI (before saturation into electron holes), the instability drives Langmuir waves — electrostatic plasma oscillations at exactly the plasma frequency f_pe. These are the fastest-growing normal mode; their growth rate is given by our Eq.18–21.

We detect them as an enhancement in the electric field power spectral density near f_pe compared to nearby frequencies. Enhancement ratio R = PSD(0.8–1.2×f_pe) / PSD(0.5–0.7×f_pe) > 3 flags active Langmuir waves.

Note: f_pe in the magnetosphere is typically 1–100 kHz, far above the EDP slow-survey Nyquist frequency (~16 Hz). This proxy works best for very high-density plasmas (f_pe < 16 Hz) or when EDP burst-mode data is available.

References: Malaspina et al. (2014) GRL 41, 5204; Kellogg et al. (2009) — Langmuir collapse; Jebaraj et al. (2025)
